It had been raining for three weeks when Aliyah first noticed that the
map was changing on its own. She had pinned it to the wall of her flat
on the eighth of March --- a battered survey chart of the Veil basin,
the kind drafted in the years when the territory still answered to
two governments --- and through the wet spring it had hung there
unremarked, a patch of brown ink and yellowed cartouche above the
kettle. It was only on the morning of the twenty-ninth that she
looked up from her coffee and saw, quite plainly, that the river had
moved.[^veil-history]

[^veil-history]: In Aliyah's defense, the Old Veil had been known to
shift its course three or four times a century even before the
cartographic incidents began; one can hardly blame her for assuming
mundane causes at first.

The Veil itself --- the river, that is, not the basin --- had spent
the previous century making a slow easterly migration of perhaps half
a kilometre per decade, a fact which had cost the village of Stonemoor
its mill and earned the village of Briar its second one. On Aliyah's
map, however, the river had since the previous evening relocated by
nearly three centimetres, which, at the chart's scale of 1:50,000,
worked out to something on the order of a kilometre and a half. The
old course was still faintly visible, the way a pencil line survives
an eraser, but the new channel was darker, more confident, and
inarguably *there.*

She did what any reasonable cartographer would have done. She made a
pot of fresh coffee, sat down on the floor in front of the map, and
took photographs at five-minute intervals for the next two hours.
By noon the river had not moved again, but a small woodland north of
Briar had acquired three new copses, and the bridge at Stonemoor
showed itself half a span longer than it had been at breakfast.

## A note on the cartographer's discipline

There is a particular vice among cartographers, well documented in the
professional literature, of preferring to be wrong than to be surprised.
A surveyor confronted with an unexpected hill will more often than not
re-walk the transit lines, recompute the bearings, and only at the end,
exhausted and out of options, accept that the hill is genuinely where
the instruments place it. The chart, after all, is meant to *represent*
the ground; if it does not, the chart is presumed at fault before the
ground is.

Aliyah's discipline was therefore badly served by what she did next,
which was to *believe the map.* She did not re-survey the Veil. She
did not write to the Department. She got out a fresh sheet of tracing
paper, laid it over the offending chart, and began to record what the
chart was doing. By the end of the week she had thirty-one sheets, a
small notebook of observations in pencil, and the dawning conviction
that the map was not so much *wrong* as *early.*

## The Department's response

The Department of Surveys received Aliyah's letter on the eleventh of
April and answered it on the fourteenth, which by the Department's
standards counted as a sprint. The reply was three sentences long and
managed in those three sentences to suggest that she might be tired,
that her instruments might be miscalibrated, and that a personal
interview would be unnecessary at this stage. She read it twice,
folded it into a paper aeroplane, and threw it into the kettle.

She then bought a train ticket to the capital.

The train left Stonemoor at six in the morning and reached the capital
shortly after noon. Aliyah spent the journey re-reading her notebook
and, somewhere between the second and third hour, realised that the
*pattern* of the map's changes was not random at all. The river had
moved exactly as it would move if the easterly drift of the past
century continued at the same rate for another forty years. The
copses north of Briar matched, almost tree for tree, a plantation
proposed in a 1987 forestry white paper that had never been funded.
The bridge at Stonemoor was the bridge the council had voted against
in 2003. The map was not predicting the future, exactly. It was
showing the futures that had been *thought about* and then *not
chosen.*

By the time the train pulled into the capital, Aliyah had stopped
being frightened and started being curious. This, in retrospect, was
the moment at which her life ceased to belong to her.

## What the archives knew

The Department's reading room was on the fourth floor of a building
that had originally been a bank, and the strongroom in the basement
still smelled faintly of pound notes and disappointment. The duty
archivist, a man named Petersen, listened to Aliyah's account with
the same expression he might have worn for a complaint about a
mislabelled folder.

"Eighty-six," he said, when she had finished.

"I'm sorry?"

"You're the eighty-sixth person to come in here with one of those.
The first was in 1923. There are seventeen on the shelves downstairs
and another fifty-odd that we know about from correspondence." He
took off his glasses, polished them on his cardigan, and put them
back on. "We don't talk about it because there isn't anything useful
to say. The maps do what they do. We file them. Sometimes the futures
they show come true and sometimes they don't. Mostly they don't."

Aliyah sat down rather more heavily than she had intended.

"Why don't they come true?" she said.

Petersen looked at her for a long moment.

"Because someone reads the map," he said, "and changes their mind."
