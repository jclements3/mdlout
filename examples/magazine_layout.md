When Eleanor Whitcombe first walked into the workshop in the autumn
of 2023, she was carrying two things: a battered hardshell case
containing the prototype of what she would later call the Lyrebird
*Concert Mark IV*, and a dog-eared spiral notebook full of measurements
that she had been adding to since her postdoc at Stanford. The case
went on a workbench under a north-facing skylight. The notebook went
into a drawer. Two years later, the prototype is the production
instrument that more than thirty professional harpists now use, and
the notebook -- mostly forgotten in that drawer -- has become a
quiet bestseller within a small community of acoustic researchers who
trade scanned pages on a private mailing list.

This is the story of a small instrument and the people who keep
remaking it. It is also the story of why, exactly, the pedal harp has
resisted the standard innovations that swept through every other
orchestral string family in the twentieth century -- and what happens
when a generation of engineers, performers, and luthiers finally
decides that the resistance has gone on long enough.

## A workshop, and a problem

The Lyrebird workshop occupies the second floor of a converted
button factory in Cambridge, Massachusetts. From the street it is
unremarkable: brick, two stories, a small brass plate by the door
that reads *Lyrebird Acoustics, Inc.* in a font so understated that
half the visitors I observed walked past it twice. Inside, the smell
is of cedar shavings and freshly cut spruce, with an under-note of
hide glue warming on a hot plate. The workshop is open to the public
on Wednesday afternoons; on the day I visited there were two
high-school students and an emeritus professor of art history
sitting in folding chairs by the tuning bench, watching Whitcombe
voice a row of newly strung wires.

> "The whole instrument was designed in 1810, and almost nothing has
> changed since," Whitcombe told me, plucking a string with the side
> of her thumbnail. "If you handed an Erard pedal harp from 1815 to
> a player today, they could tune it up and play a recital that
> afternoon. That is *amazing*, but it is also a problem."

The problem, in Whitcombe's telling, is that "almost nothing has
changed" actually means "almost nothing was *measured*". The pedal
harp's neck-to-body angle, the soundboard taper, the string
diameters, even the action of the pedal mechanism -- all of these
were settled by trial, error, and the conservative tastes of a small
group of nineteenth-century makers, and then frozen in place by the
prestige of the instruments those makers produced. There is no
modern body of measurement that says *the soundboard should be 3.2
millimetres at the bass end and 1.6 at the treble*; there are only
the instruments themselves, and the centuries of imitation that
followed.

!!! note "What "voicing" means"
    *Voicing* is the luthier's term for adjusting an instrument
    after it is built so that each string speaks evenly and the
    tonal colour is consistent across the range. On a harp, voicing
    is mostly a matter of micro-adjusting the soundboard tension and
    the position of the *pin-block* (the wooden bar that holds the
    tuning pegs). A skilled voicer can spend two full days on a
    single instrument; an unskilled one can spend two minutes.

## The notebook

The drawer-bound notebook contains, by my rough count, about eleven
hundred individual measurements. Soundboard impedance plots taken
at sixteen different points on twenty different instruments. String
break-angle photographs in dozens of frames per instrument. Pedal
action force-curves -- the relationship between the player's foot
pressure and the resulting pitch change -- recorded as ASCII text
files dumped from a sensor rig that Whitcombe and her then-supervisor
built out of a kitchen scale and a piece of bicycle inner tube.

Most of the notebook is, by Whitcombe's own admission, "noise". A
particular string sounded slightly sharper than expected; a particular
soundboard had a resonance at 380 Hz that no other soundboard in the
sample had. Treated as individual data points the entries are not
especially illuminating. But aggregated -- and Whitcombe spent three
months in the winter of 2022 aggregating them, on a borrowed laptop
in her childhood bedroom in Salem, Oregon -- they begin to tell a
story. The story is that the harps of the pedal-harp tradition are
each idiosyncratic in *small* ways, but extremely consistent in
*large* ones. The neck-to-body angle is essentially identical across
the sample. The soundboard taper falls into one of two distinct
patterns. The string-tension profile -- pounds of tension per
millimetre of string diameter, plotted against pitch -- is so similar
across the twenty instruments measured that the curves overlay each
other within the thickness of a pencil line.

> "It was the consistency that surprised me," Whitcombe said. "I
> went into the project assuming that I would find these huge
> variations between makers, and that the variations would explain
> why some harps sound the way they do and others sound differently.
> What I found instead was the opposite. The harps that sound very
> different have *almost the same measurements*. The differences are
> all in the last one percent."

## Why a feature article?

I wrote this article because the pedal harp is an instrument that
hides in plain sight. It appears in every major orchestral score
written after 1860, contributes to dozens of film scores per year,
and supports a small but devoted community of soloists. It is also
the most physically complex single instrument an orchestra owns: a
modern concert harp has 47 strings, seven double-action pedals, a
linkage system involving more than two thousand individual
components, and a body weighing close to ninety pounds.

The instrument is too important to leave to its own conservatism.
What Whitcombe and her colleagues are doing in the Cambridge
workshop -- and what a handful of other workshops in Italy, Germany,
and Japan are doing in parallel -- is the slow, patient work of
turning a craft into a discipline. They are measuring what was never
measured. They are publishing what was never published. They are
producing the kind of *quantitative ground truth* that every other
orchestral instrument family acquired in the second half of the
twentieth century, and that the pedal harp, alone among the
strings, never did.

## The next generation

I spent three days at the Lyrebird workshop. I met the apprentice
Marcus Ito, who is twenty-two and learning the trade after a
computer-science degree and two years at a robotics startup. I met
the senior voicer Yuki Tanaka, who came over from a workshop in
Saitama in 2019 and has the calmest hands I have ever watched at a
workbench. I met the cellist-turned-acoustician Henri Fabre, who is
on sabbatical from a Paris conservatory and whose laptop is covered
with stickers from open-source acoustic software projects. I sat in
on three voicing sessions, two tuning sessions, and one extended
conversation between Whitcombe and Tanaka about whether the
*nineteenth* pedal coupling -- a notoriously balky link in the
double-action mechanism -- could be replaced with a tensioned cable
instead of the traditional metal rod.

> "I want my children to inherit an instrument that has been
> *understood*," Whitcombe said on my last afternoon, putting the
> *Mark IV* back in its case after a recital-pitch tuning. "Not just
> an instrument that has been *played*."

It is too early to say whether she will succeed. The Lyrebird Mark
IV is now in the hands of thirty-three professional harpists; the
acoustic-measurements project that grew out of the spiral notebook
has been adopted, in part, by the Conservatoire National Superieur
de Musique in Paris. But the pedal harp is a slow instrument, and
the traditions it embodies are slow traditions. Whatever the
generation now in the Lyrebird workshop produces, it will take
fifty years to be sure.

In the meantime there is the work. There are the measurements. There
is the small community of luthiers and engineers and players who
trade scanned notebook pages on a private mailing list, and who --
when I asked Whitcombe whether the list had a name -- told me, with
a small smile, that of course it did, but that she would prefer I
not print it. Some things, she said, are still small enough to want
to stay small.

## A reading list

- *The Modern Concert Harp*, by Marisol Aguilar. The standard
  textbook on twentieth-century harp making.

- *Acoustic Measurements for Luthiers*, by Henri Fabre. The book
  that grew out of Fabre's open-source software project.

- *The Pedal Harp: An Encyclopedia*, by various authors. A
  comprehensive reference, in five volumes.

- *Strings Attached*, a documentary film by Cassie Yu (2024). A
  90-minute portrait of three harpists at three different points in
  their careers.
