# The First Letter

```lout
@LP
@CentredDisplay @I {
"The sea writes its letters in salt, and salt forgets nothing."
}
@CentredDisplay { --- Eleni Markou, @I { Coastal Notebooks } (1962) }
@LP
```

The first letter arrived on a Tuesday, which struck Halloran as appropriate;
Tuesdays were the kind of weekday on which extraordinary things were allowed
to happen without anyone making a fuss about them. He had walked down to the
post office at the usual hour, exchanged the usual two sentences with Mrs.
Devlin behind the counter, and accepted the usual small handful of
correspondence --- a gas bill, a circular from the parish council, a postcard
from his sister --- plus, this once, a single creamy envelope addressed in a
hand he did not recognise.[^hand]

[^hand]: Halloran kept all his correspondence in a wooden file box in the
hall cupboard; in later years, when the box was opened for the inquest, the
envelope was the only item in it whose handwriting could not be matched to
any known correspondent.

The envelope bore no stamp and no return address. It had been hand-delivered,
presumably, though by whom Mrs. Devlin could not say --- she had found it on
the floor inside the door that morning, with the morning's pile of letters
fanned around it as if it had been there first and the others had arrived to
keep it company.[^devlin] He took the letter home, set it on the kitchen
table beside the teapot, and looked at it for a long time before opening it.

[^devlin]: Mrs. Devlin's testimony at the inquest was, as one might expect,
the subject of considerable scrutiny. She maintained, against all
reasonable pressure, that the envelope had been *under* the others when
she swept them up, and that she had not noticed it until she sorted them
on the counter.

Inside was a single sheet of the same creamy paper, folded in three. The
message on it was four lines long. It said:

```lout
@LP
@CentredDisplay @I {
The tide comes in at four.  //
Bring the lantern from the shed.  //
Do not bring the dog.  //
H.
}
@LP
```

Halloran was not, by inclination or by trade, a man for whom four-line
anonymous letters at the kitchen table were a routine event. He had served
two years at sea in his twenties and forty in the postal service after
that, and the largest mystery he had ever been asked to entertain was the
question of why the morning bag from Killybegs was occasionally a parcel
short.[^postal] He read the letter once, then a second time, then put on
his coat and walked down to the strand.

[^postal]: The Killybegs short-parcel mystery, incidentally, was resolved
in 1987 when a retiring sorter confessed to having kept a Christmas
hamper meant for a customer he disliked.

## A second arrival

```lout

@CentredDisplay @I {

}
@CentredDisplay { --- Anonymous, @I { The Coastal Office Manual } (1903) }
```

The second letter arrived on Friday, in the same hand, in the same creamy
envelope, again with no stamp and again, Mrs. Devlin maintained, sitting
on the floor inside the door when she opened up. This one was longer ---
half a page --- and contained instructions of a more specific
character.[^second] Halloran read it standing in the kitchen with his
coat still on, the kettle ticking softly to itself behind him as it
cooled, and when he had finished reading he sat down rather heavily in
the chair by the door.

[^second]: The text of the second letter survives in a transcription
made by Halloran's nephew some years later; the original, like the
first, was not among the effects recovered from the cottage.

The instructions were these: that he should walk to the headland at
moonrise on the night of the spring tide; that he should carry the
lantern, unlit, and a length of clean rope; that he should wait at the
mark of high water until he heard a particular sound, which the letter
described only as *"the sound of the sea remembering its own name"*;
and that he should then do exactly as he was told, by whomever told
him, without question and without delay.

The letter was signed, again, with the single initial *H.*

Halloran, who was himself an H., put the kettle back on.

The third letter, by contrast, is the only one of which a copy
survives.[^copy] It was found among Halloran's papers after his
disappearance, folded inside the back cover of his service diary
from his years at sea, and is reproduced here in full for the first
time. It reads:

[^copy]: The original is held in the Special Collections of the County
Library at Donegal; access is by appointment and at the discretion of
the keeper.

```lout

"To the keeper of the lantern."
//
"What you have been doing on the headland for the last forty years has"
//
"been noticed, and is appreciated.  Do not stop.  H."
}
@LP
```

The third letter is dated three days after the date of Halloran's
disappearance.

## Section summary

The three letters together constitute the only direct evidence of the
events at the headland during the autumn of 1973. The first letter
established the appointment; the second established the manner of it;
the third, with its impossible date, established that whatever it was
Halloran had been doing on the headland, he was still understood, by
someone or something, to be doing it. The inquest, predictably,
returned an open verdict. The lantern was found on the strand at
first light, unlit, with the wick trimmed and the glass clean. The
rope was never recovered.
